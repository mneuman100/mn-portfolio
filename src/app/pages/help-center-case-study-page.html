Background

At Origami Risk, the Billing section in the Help Center had an existing structure, but it did not reflect how users approach billing tasks. The 45 topics were scattered across four folders with no meaningful subcategories in any of them, mixing unrelated workflows and making it difficult for users to locate specific content. The Risk Management Information System (RMIS) section also had content gaps and topics that needed to be reorganized to better reflect current product functionality.

This research was initiated to map the full scope of content gaps across both areas, prioritize them with stakeholder input, and establish a repeatable framework for ongoing gap identification and prioritization.

A Senior Client Executive's email in July 2025 confirmed clients were experiencing the same gaps the research had already identified.

Research questions

  1. What content is missing from the Billing and RMIS Help Center sections relative to the actual product functionality?
  2. Is the existing content organized in a way that reflects how users think about billing and RMIS tasks?
  3. Which gaps represent the highest priority for users based on stakeholder input?
  4. Does reorganizing content into logical sections make it easier for users to locate information without SME assistance?

Method

Phase 1: Content audit

Conducted a three-source content audit for both Billing and RMIS: compared existing (1) Help Center topics against (2) the Product Listing spreadsheet and (3) release notes to identify missing, outdated, and out of place content. Documented findings into two structured gap analysis spreadsheets for Billing and RMIS.

Phase 2: Stakeholder interviews and prioritization

Collaborated with Product Managers for each area to validate taxonomy, confirm correct section names, identify additional gaps not captured in the audit, and establish a priority order for remediation. Used stakeholder prioritization to build a sprint-based documentation schedule.

Phase 3: IA redesign and content planning

Redesigned the Billing section information architecture from four unstructured folders into a six-area framework built around how users actually approach billing tasks. The RMIS section was reorganized from five folders to seven folders, with three new sections introduced to group related functionality that had previously been scattered.

For each completed sprint, I conducted a deep content inventory of the live product to identify what information was missing in the Help Center relative to actual software functionality, then created a full content plan with time estimates and a sprint schedule for each area. Remaining sprint areas have been identified and prioritized, with the content plan ready for continuation.

Phase 4 — Execution and validation

Three documentation sprints were completed from the research plan. A client-reported gap, submitted four months after the audit began, independently confirmed the research had identified real user needs ahead of formal feedback.

Following the IA redesign, the logical next research step would have been to validate the new structure with users. Findings

Information Architecture: The existing Billing structure scattered 45 topics across four separate folders with no task-oriented subcategories. The reorganization involved more than adding new content. Some topics were merged to eliminate redundancy, others were moved out of Billing entirely, and additional missing content was added. The result was six logical sections containing 50 topics, structured to reflect how users actually approach billing tasks rather than how content had historically accumulated. The RMIS section was reorganized from five folders to seven folders, with three new sections added to group related functionality.

Content Gaps: Significant content gaps existed across both areas. The audit identified existing functionality without topics in the Help Center, content covered in release notes that had never been incorporated into Help Center topics, and existing topics that were incomplete relative to current functionality. For example, direct bill payment workflows had no dedicated topic in the Help Center despite being a core billing function. This specific gap was later confirmed by an internal review conducted on behalf of a client.

Validation: An internal Senior Client Executive's review submitted in July 2025 independently identified the same gaps found in the March 2025 audit, prompted by a client request for general Help Center documentation. The review specifically identified: missing direct bill workflows, unclear agency billing content, and organizational confusion from topics appearing in multiple sections. My research identified the same user needs four months before they were surfaced through internal client feedback.
