Context

At Origami Risk, I evaluated two AI tools, Guru AI and Command AI, for potential adoption across the organization in 2024. Both were in active pilot phases and needed a closer look before anyone committed to purchasing or deploying them more broadly. My job was to dig into how well they actually worked, from the perspective of the documentation team and the clients who would ultimately use them.

Background

Guru AI would be an internal-facing tool used in both GURU, our knowledge management system, and SLACK, our workplace communication tool.

Command AI would be a client-facing tool used to source information from our Help Center articles.

The goal was to assess each tool before any organizational commitment was made.

Research questions

Guru AI

Is Guru AI ready for internal adoption at Origami, and what guardrails would be needed for safe use?

Command AI

Can this tool surface Help Center content accurately, or will it mislead clients with incorrect or fabricated answers?

Method

Guru AI

I started by sitting down with Sarah Dantino a Business Process Manager, at Origami, who was running the pilot, to understand how the tool was configured, what it could access, and where the guardrails were. Then I tested it myself across multiple sessions, asking real questions, documenting where it got things wrong, and capturing screenshots of specific failures. My findings went back to Sarah as part of her ongoing assessment.

Command AI

I coordinated and participated in a collaborative evaluation with four members of the documentation team, running from August 30 to September 13, 2024. Each of us submitted real questions and rated every response against six criteria: whether it was correct, where the answer came from, whether it was a hallucination, how well it sourced content, whether the suggested follow-up questions made sense, and whether it covered the right topics. We tracked everything in a shared spreadsheet.

What we found, sorted by risk category

Hallucinations

Found in both tools, with different severity

Guru AI

Suggested answers sometimes did not match the source card it cited and the AI appeared to generalize from unrelated content. Other times, the tool returned an answer referencing terms that never appeared in the cited source. I noted during testing that "If I didn't check my source, it would be easy to take this as the correct answer." The false-confidence risk was the most concerning pattern in the Guru AI evaluation: where the answer sounds authoritative but the cited source doesn't support it.

Command AI

51% of evaluated responses (22 of 43) were incorrect. With over half of the responses returning incorrect information, the most severe answers involved the tool generating entirely fabricated content for topics not documented in the Help Center, but presenting the answer with the same confident tone as accurate responses. In one case, the system offered a suggested follow-up question for content that did not exist, then generated false answers when the evaluator selected it.

A complex question about billing configuration received a response that was "dangerously incomplete": Steps were omitted while the tool's authoritative tone implied completeness. Also, if asked the same question three times, the AI produced three different answers with varying numbers of source links, lowering users' confidence in the tool and causing uncertainty.

Accuracy

Different failure patterns in each tool

Guru AI

Accuracy varied and was heavily dependent on the content quality. When the tool found a matching card, answers were generally useful. A live test by a colleague confirmed that AI supplied the correct answer faster than it would take asking the same question to a SME and waiting for response. However, accuracy degraded significantly when the source content was outdated. This turned out to be a configuration limitation issue since Guru AI searches all unarchived cards regardless of age, including content from our Evernote migration era that had never been updated. This meant that old, potentially invalid content was being returned with the same confidence as current, verified content.

Command AI

The AI was 40% correct, 51% incorrect, and 9% not applicable across 43 evaluated questions. The accuracy was the lowest when asking complex or multi-step questions and highest when asking simple definition questions. One evaluator noted during testing that "the more generic the question, the more likely Command AI is to be wrong."

Sourcing

A risk in both tools but for different reasons

Guru AI

The tool sometimes cited source cards that did not contain the information it returned, meaning the cited source and the generated answer didn't match. This created a specific risk: users who didn't manually verify the source would accept incorrect answers as validated by the tool. After an update, I also discovered that the platform removed the option for giving "possible sources" when AI found "No direct answer" which greatly reduced users' ability to find related content when the AI couldn't answer directly.

Command AI

The tool consistently drew from sources outside the Help Center, which was the root cause of most hallucinations. When we asked the vendor directly whether the AI could be limited to Help Center content only, the answer was no. The sourcing problem was architectural and not something we could configure around. That made it a serious risk. The tool also linked to incorrect topics, dropped relevant links entirely, and in one case, linked to a Command AI website URL that returned a 404 error.

Reliability

Structural limits in Guru AI, interface failures in Command AI

Guru AI

Reliability was affected by a manual sync process which refreshed the AI's Slack knowledge bank. But the knowledge bank was limited to only the last 90 days of Slack history and the process could take up to six hours. There was also a 25 Slack channel limit, which meant not all relevant Slack content could be included in searches. Content staleness from outdated Guru cards was also a persistent concern. The tool returned old and potentially invalid cards alongside current, validated cards listing them with equal importance. Despite these limitations, the tool demonstrated sufficient reliability for internal use if we adopt appropriate user education and maintain content standards.

Command AI

Reliability failures were both content-based and UI-based. Responses disappeared after periods of inactivity, a resize bug made the chat interface unusable until it was closed and then reopened, and suggested follow-up questions were displayed overtop of the primary answer when scrolling. Varied answers to the same questions further undermined trust in the tool as a consistent reference.

Findings

Guru AI

Guru AI came out as a viable tool for internal use if the right guardrails were in place. We saw it work well in practice when a colleague found a correct answer through the AI faster than a SME could respond directly. We would need to implement conditions for safe use, including: verifying your sources, being aware that older Guru cards may contain outdated information, and knowing that the tool only searches back 90 days of Slack history. To get the best results out of Guru AI, outdated Guru cards would need to be archived or updated, and the team would need to remain committed to maintaining content consistently.

Command AI

Command AI was not ready for adoption in the state we evaluated it. The evaluation gave us 49 documented questions with specific failure commentary to back that up. We also identified the clearest path forward: if the tool could be constrained to use our Help Center as its primary source, accuracy and reliability would likely improve significantly.

Outcome

Guru AI

The Guru AI findings went back to Sarah Dantino as part of her ongoing pilot assessment and helped inform the conversation about whether to move forward with a broader rollout.

Command AI

The Command AI evaluation provided a documented, evidence-based case for pausing adoption, not rejecting it outright. The 51% incorrect rate, the sourcing architecture limitation, and the UI issues were all specific and defensible. More importantly, the evaluation laid out a clear path forward: constrain the sourcing to Help Center content, resolve the UI reliability issues, then reconsider.
